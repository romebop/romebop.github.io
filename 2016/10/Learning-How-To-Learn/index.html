Learning How To Learn

Oct 1, 2016

One big topic on my mind lately has been on productivity. I want to be a prolific person, learning (input) & producing (output) as much as I can within the limited time I have. So I decided I would do some meta learning and took the time to take a course called Learning How To Learn. It’s offered on Coursera and taught by a wonderful instructor named Dr. Barbara Oakley. I’d like to share some of the lessons I’ve found interesting.

Focusing and Diffusing

As humans, we experience two modes of learning: Focused & Diffused.

Focused learning is our traditional idea of learning: through concentrating your mind on the subject. It’s like traveling along a familiar paved road, and actively trying to break down any barriers along the way.

Diffused learning on the other hand, is learning in a much broader scope. We often learn in diffuse mode, as we step away from the focus at hand and take a break or move onto something else. This allows our subconscious brain to make broader neural connections and thus examine the problem in a new light. If you’ve ever been stuck on a problem and sometime later come to a solution in the middle of another seemingly irrelevant task, you’ve experienced diffuse mode learning. The famous physicist Richard Feynman has stories of solving physics problems in his sleep and writing them down after waking up. (lol what a baws)

What’s interesting here is that we can’t be in both thinking modes at the same time. If we find ourselves facing a seemingly impenetrable wall despite long periods of focus, it’s best to take a step back and let our diffuse mode of learning take over. This fine tuned balance between focused and diffused modes of learning, as we encounter new problems to solve, is key to maintaining a healthy forward momentum.

Procrastinating

Procrastination is a super infamous hindrance to productivity, for some a more serious concern than to others. It happens to follow a predictable pattern:

  1. encounter an unhappy feeling : (
  2. funnel attention to a more pleasant task : D
  3. feel happy (…only temporarily) t_t

Interestingly, research has shown that the discomfort, or unhappy feeling that arises from anticipating a task, soon disappears once you start. This means that our primary battle against procrastination lies in those precarious initial moments before beginning.

One method that has proven to be useful in this battle is known as Pomodoro Technique. The Pomodoro Technique is a time management method that simply involves the use of a 25 minute timer to commit to a given task, which is then followed by a short break. Making a contract with ourselves that we would commit to a task for 25 minutes (no more, no less), is an effective strategy in combatting the discomfort cue that too often devolves into procrastination.

Shifting our focus in such a way to committing to a time duration of work, rather than committing to complete an entire task in one sitting, allows us to move from a product oriented mind set into a process oriented mind set. A process oriented mind set allows us to more easily step into the flow of work, and thus become more productive.

and More

There were loads more topics discussed including effective memory management, the process of developing habits, and exercise, but these were the two ideas that stood out to me.

Let’s look forward to a more knowledge-filled and productive future!
